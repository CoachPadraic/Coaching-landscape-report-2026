The picture is one of conditional optimism: confidence that tracks security, pathways that stall in mid-career, and a workforce asking, above all, for standards.

01The economics of coaching

Most coaches combine several roles, not one full-time job.

For most respondents, coaching is several part-time, self-employed and voluntary roles rather than a single position — held together by the coach’s own time.

What the data shows

Only around one in five respondents (21%) hold a single full-time paid coaching role. A third (36%) are self-employed or freelance, 15% are volunteers, and the rest combine part-time pay with voluntary hours — a pattern consistent even among coaches with 16+ years in the field.

Why it matters

Insecure income shapes who can stay in the profession, limits how much coaches invest in their own development, and pushes experienced people out at the point they are most valuable to the system.

What would help

  • Written contracts with clubs, not informal arrangements.
  • Roles shared across schools, clubs and counties so several part-time positions form one sustainable job.
  • Business and pricing skills inside coach education.
  • Realistic guidance on what a viable coaching income looks like in Ireland.
“The ability to generate enough income to sustain a normal lifestyle without having to work 60+ hours a week.” Self-employed coach · Club sport · 3–5 years

02Pathways & mid-career

Pathways are unclear, and progression stalls in mid-career.

Coaches can plan the next session but struggle to see the next five years. The route from volunteer to sustainable career is unclear at entry and constrained in the middle.

What the data shows

‘Career pathways for coaches in Ireland’ was the most-requested learning topic — chosen by 60% of respondents, ahead of every technical subject. Optimism rises through the early career, peaks at 84% at 6–10 years, then falls to 50% at 11–15 years — the lowest of any group.

Why it matters

Several respondents described progression as relationship-driven rather than transparent. New coaches struggled to secure a first opportunity; experienced coaches saw few senior roles and little room to advance without moving abroad.

What would help

  • Transparent recruitment for county and development roles.
  • Internships and placements that lead to employment.
  • Structured mentoring that runs in both directions.
  • Visible examples of coaches who built sustainable careers in Ireland.
03Standards & accreditation

Coaches’ first priority for change is standards.

Asked for one change that would improve the profession, the largest group did not ask for funding or recognition first. They asked for standards.

What the data shows

Around a third of those who answered — the largest group by some distance — called for some form of professional regulation: accredited or chartered status, protected job titles, minimum qualification standards for roles, or mandatory CPD.

Why it matters

Respondents described unqualified practitioners working under the same titles as those who trained for years, with clubs and committees unable to tell quality apart. Where those hiring can’t judge quality, standards, rates and trust drift downward together.

What would help — a tiered approach

  • Minimum standards matched to the responsibility of the role.
  • A recognised register that values verified experience alongside qualifications.
  • Title protection for the most safety-critical roles first.
  • Education for those who recruit coaches.
“Everybody is calling themselves a strength & conditioning coach, often with no qualifications and delivering poor practice.” S&C coach · Academy / pathway · 6–10 years

Coaches develop mainly through experience and peers.

What the data shows

On-the-job experience is the main source of development (65%), followed by short courses / CPD (52%) and mentors / peers (52%). Just under half (49%) named formal qualifications, and podcasts (42%) now rival research papers (40%). The most-needed areas are practical coaching skills and youth athlete development (21% each), with business and career development third.

Why it matters

The informal system has two weaknesses. It is uneven — depending heavily on who happens to be around a coach — and it is costly, with coaches funding courses, travel and time out of already limited incomes.

What would help

  • Build development around how coaches already learn.
  • Structured mentoring and peer groups.
  • Short, applied, affordable CPD with chances to practise.
  • Recognition that gives formal credit for verified on-the-job learning.
“Coaching is a skill that constantly needs self-improvement and reflection. Every coach, at any level, can work towards being better. It doesn’t just happen.” Sports coach · Club sport · 16+ years
05People & technology

Human skills are expected to matter most in an AI era.

Coaches don’t see AI replacing them. They expect it to raise the value of what it cannot do: connecting with another person.

What the data shows

Asked which skills will matter most over the next five years, more than half pointed to communication, rapport, empathy and relationship-building. Around a third named AI, data and technology — almost always alongside, rather than instead of, human skills.

Why it matters

Respondents flagged a risk of a two-tier workforce: coaches who can use data and AI to extend their impact, and coaches without the time or means to build that fluency. Some warned that entry-level analysis roles may be absorbed by technology, raising the bar for a first job.

What would help

Treat AI and data literacy as part of basic coach education — short, applied, plain-language — paired with deliberate development of the communication and listening skills coaches themselves rank first.

“Communication — engaging with athletes at a human level and not getting lost in AI.” Volunteer coach · Club sport · 16+ years

Why it matters

Read together, the optimism rests more on coaches’ belief in themselves than in the system. That makes it an asset — but a fragile one. Experienced coaches who conclude the economics don’t work take both their optimism and their expertise with them.

What would help

The levers are those named throughout this report: secure and combinable paid roles, transparent pathways, recognised standards, and affordable applied development. Moving these would make the 73% a measure of a profession that works, rather than of personal resilience.

“The bar for full-time roles is growing all the time, leaving only those with external financial means able to sustain it.” S&C coach · High performance · 11–15 years
Where next

Implications for the sector.

1

Make the economics work first

No single club can sustain a full-time role — but clubs, schools and counties acting together can. This points to shared appointments, multi-club contracts and realistic costing. Until the economics work, other improvements lose talent.

2

Make coaching pathways visible

Publish how roles are filled and create internships that lead to employment. Mid-career coaches — the least optimistic group — need visible progression that does not require moving abroad.

3

Begin standards work at sector level

A chartered profession may take time, but club-level awareness can be built sooner. Support those who recruit, develop a voluntary register that recognises verified experience, and protect the most safety-critical titles first.

4

Support development the way coaches learn

Coaches develop through experience, peers and short applied formats. Investment should reflect that — mentoring, practice-based CPD and continuing communities — with data and AI literacy as core coach education at every level.

The through-line

Irish coaches value the work and believe in its future — but many are funding that future themselves, through second jobs, unpaid hours and personal cost. The measures they identified are practical, affordable and largely within the sector’s control: secure and combinable roles, visible pathways, recognised standards, and applied development built around how coaches actually learn.

Next steps — who can act, and when
This season

Clubs — recruit against simple standards, put basic contracts in writing, and explore sharing one coach across clubs.

This year

Coaching networks — run short, applied, affordable CPD; launch two-way mentoring; publish this report annually as a benchmark.

This cycle

Governing bodies — publish transparent role criteria and pathways, and pilot a register that values verified experience alongside qualifications.

Ongoing

Coaches — pair people skills with plain-language data and AI literacy, and seek mentors both above and below their level.

Methodology.
The Irish Coaching Landscape Survey 2026 was run online by the ISCN and received 81 responses from coaches and performance practitioners working in Ireland. It combined fixed-choice questions with open-text questions on challenges, future skills and desired change. Open-text answers were coded by theme; where a percentage refers to a theme, the base is the number who answered that question (71 for challenges, 68 for skills, 66 for ‘one change’) and should be read as directional.

Reading the numbers.
With 81 respondents, subgroup figures involve small samples and should be read as directional patterns rather than precise population estimates. Quotes are reproduced from responses, lightly edited for spelling, and anonymised. Respondents skew toward strength & conditioning and performance roles, reflecting the ISCN community — a relevant lens, but not a census of all Irish coaching.

On method. Analysis, drafting and design were supported by AI tools; the survey design, interpretation and all findings are the work of ISCN.
